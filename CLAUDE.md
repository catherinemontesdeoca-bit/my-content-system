# Intuit Content Design Guidelines

Source: Intuit Content Design System (contentdesign.intuit.com), compiled July 15, 2025.

---

## Foundations

### Emotional Design

Designs that evoke emotions build meaningful relationships with customers. We empathize, reassure, and sometimes surprise and delight — without overpromising, manipulating, or being saccharine.

**Put customer benefits above business goals**

- Focus on what helps customers prosper, not revenue metrics
- ✅ "We can help you grow your small business—and we'll be with you every step of the way"
- ❌ "Have you tried this amazing new feature from our world-class product? Only an extra $35/mo"

**Watch your tone**

- Respect customers' moods; don't tell them how to feel
- Acknowledge when something might be challenging
- ✅ "We know change can be hard. We're here for you."
- ❌ "Great news! Your payroll system is modernizing." (don't assume change is good news)
- ✅ "We simplify every part of getting paid, so you can focus on what matters." (lead with benefit)
- ❌ "You're going to love our payroll solution!" (don't tell customers how they'll feel)
- Use "don't worry" only in low-stakes situations. If removing it doesn't break the content, cut it.

**Beware of superlatives**

- Superlatives (most, best, top, fastest, ultimate, premier, most-trusted) erode trust
- Don't use "world-class" — if you're world class, you don't have to say it
- "Good" often works better than "great"
- Facts beat superlatives — cite sources for bold claims
- ✅ "We'll take good care of your team"
- ❌ "We'll take great care of your team"
- ✅ "Pay your team with the #1 payroll service provider" (only with cited data)

**Make the most of microcopy**

- Don't miss small content moments (under 20 seconds to read)
- A human-sounding voice with an opportunity to discover is compelling
- ✅ "We use cookies for sign in and checkout. Learn more" (but note: "Learn" can sound like a burden — make it feel light)

### Content Testing

Testing builds confidence in content design decisions. Any test, however small, teaches you something.

**Know what you want to learn**

Before testing, answer:
- What do I want to learn?
- How will the outcome benefit the customer?
- How will I measure success?

If you can't articulate how the outcome benefits the customer, don't run the test.

**Go broad to learn big**

- Don't just tweak words — frame two or more different approaches
- Be bold: compare benefits-first vs. purely functional copy
- Ask: What's the underlying customer problem? How might this change customer behavior?

**Qualitative vs. quantitative**

| Type | When to use |
|---|---|
| Quantitative | Learn *how* users act; validate at scale; quantify a problem |
| Qualitative | Learn *why* users act; usability/comprehension tests; build empathy |

**Test types**

- Customer interviews
- Card sorting (for naming menus or labels)
- A/B test (at scale, after qualitative insights)
- Money test (to identify which benefits resonate)
- Highlighting (to diagnose tone issues)

**Always write a research plan.** Include: goals, target customer, hypothesis, and script.

### Writing Small

Less is (almost always) more. Customers come to accomplish tasks, not read.

**Principles**

- Make it scannable — most people skim
- Use less content — fewer words = lower cognitive load
- Keep it simple — complicated pages look complicated and intimidating
- Focus on what's useful — customers read only what's important to them
- Guide with structure — use hierarchy, bullets, and visual space

**Headlines**

Tight and specific. Quickly convey the gist and let readers decide to read more or skip.
- ✅ "Get expert help with Full Service"
- ❌ "You told us you wanted to hand your taxes off to a tax expert. Ready to continue?"

**Front-load key messages**

Per Nielsen Norman Group, people scan — put critical content first. Use headlines and CTAs to flag important information. For modals, the CTA often does the informational heavy lifting.

**Progressive disclosure**

Break long flows into bite-sized pieces. Especially helpful for:
- Long forms or workflows
- Neurodivergent users (ADHD, anxiety) who may abandon a wall of text

Methods: links, accordions, conditional fields, step-throughs.

**Break it up**

Use bullets to break large text chunks into digestible pieces.

### How We Work

Small, diverse teams drive the best work — but only when everyone collaborates from the start. Include content designers, visual designers, interaction designers, product managers, researchers, and developers early. Don't bring people in after decisions are made.

## Voice and Tone

Voice is who we are. Tone is based on the customer's mood. Context is key.

### QuickBooks Voice

**Brand character:** The proactive champion — champion of those who dare to dream.

**Voice attributes:**

| Attribute | What it means |
|---|---|
| Warm | Friendly, here for customers, empathetic but not sappy, patient and approachable |
| Confident | Self-assured but not arrogant, energetic but not over the top, smart but not a know-it-all |
| Genuine | Sincere and candid, straightforward but not blunt, say what we mean |

**Examples:**
- ✅ Hi / Hey there / Thanks / You need to / Heads up
- ❌ Greetings / Dear / Thank you / Salutations / You're required to / Warning

**Tone spectrum (emotional → functional):**

| More emotional | More functional |
|---|---|
| Header, email subject line, body content | Tooltip, CTA, toast message |
| Subhead, email preheader, help content | Error message, bulleted list, inline message |

**Content strategy principles:**
1. It's about them, not us — focus on customers, not ourselves
2. Speak their language — human-to-human, everyday words
3. Focus on the payoff — explain *why*, not just *what*
4. Keep it simple — every word must earn its place
5. Delight when it's right — celebrate at the right moment

### Mailchimp Voice

**Brand character:** The expert absurdist — ingenuity and subversion combined.

- **Expert:** Business acumen, intelligence, precision
- **Absurdist:** Subversive, radical, strange, compelling

**Voice principles:**

| Principle | What it means |
|---|---|
| Clear communicator | Concise and direct; provokes emotion without sacrificing clarity |
| Empathetic persuader | Precise solutions with empathy, especially around business priorities |
| Confident expert | Makes the difficult actually easy, not just "look easy"; inspires and demystifies |
| Real person | Tell it like it is; clear, human conversations over sly humor |

### TurboTax Voice

**Brand character:** Your neighborhood tax visionary — relentlessly in the customer's corner.

**Voice principles:**

| Principle | Spontaneous contexts | Structured contexts |
|---|---|---|
| Down to earth | Add humor, be current, ground in culture | Be friendly, simplify, call it what it is |
| Honestly insightful | Lead with empathy, have their back, say it straight | Translate jargon, quantify the benefit, offer proactive solutions |
| Contagiously confident | Have swagger, be at ease, tell audacious stories | Speak with authority, back up claims, give customers control |

## Marketing

### SEO

We write for people who use search engines — not for search engines.

**Headers**
- Use one H1 per page
- Match content relevance to header hierarchy (H1 → H2 → H3)
- Keep headers natural and readable
- Don't use headers for CTAs or body-sized text

**Title tags**
- Format: `primary keyword – secondary keyword | brand name`
- Keep under 60 characters (max 70 before truncation)
- Give every page a unique title
- Put important keywords first
- No ALL CAPS; sentence case preferred
- ✅ "QuickBooks Online Pricing and Free Trial | QuickBooks"

**URLs**
- Short, readable, keyword-rich
- Use hyphens as word separators (not underscores or spaces)
- Always lowercase; no ALL CAPS or CamelCase
- No dates, codes, or random letters
- ✅ Readable for humans = readable for search engines

**Meta descriptions**
- 60–160 characters (including spaces)
- Active, compelling, natural tone
- Unique per page — don't repeat phrases across pages

---

### Blog Posts

**Style**
- Short, concise sentences and paragraphs; 6th-grade reading level
- First-person stories should read like *you* wrote them, not a brand
- Hook with a point of view and personal story — connect the topic to a human story

**Grammar**
- Short paragraphs (1–2 sentences)
- Use em dashes (—) to separate thoughts, not en dashes (–) or hyphens (-)

**Length:** 800–1,500 words (4–6 minutes reading time)

**What to write about**
- ✅ Ideas you can't easily Google
- ✅ Explorations in tech/design with an interesting hook
- ✅ Unique point of view with value readers can't find elsewhere
- ❌ Don't push Intuit products or features
- ❌ Don't just recap an event or speaking engagement

---

### App Stores

**Titles**
- iOS truncates at 23 characters; Google Play at 30
- Put the most important info or keyword first
- No typos — accuracy over character count

**Subtitles (iOS)**
- Sentence case, no all caps
- No jargon; active, benefits-first approach with a keyword

**App descriptions**
- Intro: First 170 characters are most important (visible before "more")
- Features: List 5–7 in bold, framed as benefits with active verbs
- Max 5 bullets per feature; keep them short
- Emoji in bullets: sparingly, never replacing words
- Footer: social proof, awards, testimonials, legal disclaimers, privacy statement, About Intuit

**Feature copy:**
- ✅ "View verified income and debt in one place" / "Say goodbye to late fees"
- ❌ "Single account dashboard" / "Better budgeting" (too vague)
- ✅ "Review transactions and add them to your accounting books with a tap."
- ❌ Long, run-on sentences that cram in too many features

## Talking to Customers

### Celebrations

Use celebrations sparingly — overuse makes them condescending.

**Celebrate when the customer accomplished something, not when we did:**
- ✅ Customer reached a goal they set (paid off debt, hit savings target)
- ✅ Customer achieved a major milestone (completed taxes, first payroll run, refund deposited)
- ✅ Customer sees value from a changed habit (more savings, books closed on time)
- ❌ Routine tasks (sent an invoice, turned on a preference, completed Step 2)

**Before celebrating, ask:**
- How often does this moment occur? (Daily events don't deserve celebration)
- Could a celebration undermine confidence? (e.g., celebrating a successful import implies it usually fails)
- Could this backfire? (e.g., a tax break due to medical hardship)

**Celebration vs. success message:**

| Celebrate | Success message only |
|---|---|
| Loan approved, paid off a loan, reached savings goal | Sent an invoice, categorized a trip, made progress toward a goal |
| First (or 100th) payroll run, refund deposited | Submitted loan request, turned on a preference, created a budget |

**Examples:**
- ✅ "Monique, you paid off your loan! Fantastic work!"
- ✅ "You crushed it! Congrats on paying down your $6,420 credit card debt."
- ❌ "Right on! You've signed in successfully." (too trivial)
- ❌ "Woo hoo! You sent your 57th invoice." (routine task)
- ❌ "Nice job. You made it through Step 2!" (condescending)

## Accessibility and Inclusion

### Readability

**Target:** 5th–8th grade reading level (ages 10–14).

This helps:
- Neurodivergent readers
- Non-native English speakers
- People who are distracted, rushed, or tired
- Translation and localization

**How to hit the target:**

| Tactic | Why it helps |
|---|---|
| Shorter sentences and words | People skip long words (8–9 letters); short ones (3–5 letters) get read |
| Familiar, conversational words | Use "use" not "utilize"; clear even for complex financial topics |
| Simpler verb tenses | Past/present/future; easier to process and translate |

**Examples of simpler tenses:**
- ✅ "to save" not "to be saving"
- ✅ "you import" not "you'll be importing"
- ✅ "you got" not "you had been getting"

**Tools:** Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level, Gunning Fog Index, Flesch Reading Ease (higher = better). Writer has these built in. Use them as guides, not gospel.

*Note: Technical audiences (e.g., accountants) may need higher-level terminology — that's OK, just make sure the words are clear to that specific audience.*

## Style and Usage

### Punctuation

**Ampersands (&)**
- Don't use in body text or most headers unless part of a proper noun
- Exception: navigation, tweets, chart/table headers (to save space)
- Don't use + to mean "and" (except partnerships like "QuickBooks + Square")

**Apostrophes**
- Use for contractions, possessives, and rare plurals
- Joint possession: one apostrophe only (Jack and Jill's)
- Individual possession: each gets one (Jack's and Jill's)
- ✅ Let's / They're / You'll / It's
- ❌ Ain't / Shan't / Y'all (regional contractions)
- If possessives look or sound awkward, rewrite to avoid the apostrophe

**Asterisks**
- Flags a corresponding note at the bottom of the page
- Use sparingly — they interrupt reading flow
- Placement: before a dash, after every other punctuation mark
- Legal disclaimers: 1 asterisk (*) for promos/offers; 2 asterisks (**) for features/product info
- Don't use multiple asterisks in one sentence

**Brackets**
- Angle brackets < >: rarely use; don't use > to separate UI elements
  - ✅ "Go to Taxes, then select Payroll Tax."
  - ❌ "Select Taxes > Payroll Tax"
- Square brackets [ ]: for inserting text in quotes, clarifying pronouns, or parentheticals within parentheticals

**Colons**
- Use to introduce a list when the intro text is a complete sentence
- Use to introduce examples when it aids readability

## Word List (Selected Terms)

| Term | Guidance |
|---|---|
| 401(k) | Use this format. Not 401K or 401k. |
| a.m. / p.m. | Use AM / PM (no periods, capitalized) |
| above / below | Avoid — not accessible. Refer to the exact area or element name instead. |
| accept | Prefer "add" over "accept" in QuickBooks transaction context — clearer for customers. |
| account | Lowercase. Exception: "Intuit Account" (proper noun). |
| account statement | Use "account statement," not "bank statement." |
| accounts payable (AP) | Spell out on first use. Lead with explanation: "money owed by a business to its suppliers." |
| accounts receivable (AR) | Spell out on first use. Lead with explanation: "a legally enforceable claim for payment." |
| ACH | Prefer "bank transfer" or "bank transfer (ACH)" in the US. Don't use ACH outside the US. |
| active / inactive | Use for services/subscriptions. Use "make active/inactive" for customer actions. |
| sign in (v.) / sign-in (n./adj.) | Hyphenate as noun/adjective. Two words as verb. "Sign in to" (not "sign into"). |
| sign on | Don't use. Use "sign in" instead. |
| sign out | Pair with "sign in." Don't mix "sign in" with "log out." |
| sign up (v.) / sign-up (n./adj.) | Hyphenate as noun/adjective. Two words as verb. Use for new account creation CTAs. |
| simple / simply | Avoid — simplicity is subjective. If it's simple, it should be self-evident. |
| since vs. because | Use "because" for cause and effect. "Since" is for time. |
| Single (tax status) | Capitalize when referring to a filing status. |
| Social Security number / SSN | Spell out and capitalize. Use SSN only with space constraints. |
| sold down the river | Don't use — rooted in racist history. Use "betrayed" instead. |
| sorry | Apologize only when you've genuinely failed to meet expectations. Don't use as filler. |
| specialist | OK for sales team or general customer assistance roles (without specific credentials). |

## Copy Review Checklist

1. Is it in active voice?
2. Does the CTA have a verb + outcome?
3. Is it in sentence case?
4. Is the reading level 5th–8th grade?
5. Have I removed superlatives and hype?
6. Am I telling customers how to feel — or respecting how they already feel?
7. Would a friendly, knowledgeable colleague say this out loud?
